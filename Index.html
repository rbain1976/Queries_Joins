Research Questions

Attribute Queries

1. Which census tracts in Jackson County have minority populations above the county average?

2. Which census tracts have significant population growth between 2010 and 2020?

Spatial Queries

1. Which census tracts are located within designated urban areas?

2. Which census tracts are located within 1 mile of Interstate 5?

Spatial Joins

1. What is the racial composition of each city in Jackson County?

2. How do housing patterns (ownership vs. rental) differ between urban and rural areas?

Combination Analysis

1. Which census tracts have both high minority populations and high rental rates?

2. Which census tracts exhibit both high population growth and high homeownership rates?

Data and Study Area

Study Area

Jackson County, Oregon is located in southwestern Oregon along the California border. The county encompasses 2,801 square miles of diverse landscapes, including the Rogue Valley, portions of the Cascade Range, and significant agricultural areas.

The population is primarily concentrated in the Medford metropolitan area and along the Interstate 5 corridor. With a 2020 population of approximately 223,000, Jackson County represents a diverse mix of urban, suburban, and rural communities.

Data Description

This analysis uses census tract data from the U.S. Census Bureau, including demographic variables such as:

  • Population counts
  • Racial composition
  • Gender distribution
  • Age demographics
  • Household characteristics
  • Housing tenure

Additional geographic datasets include city boundaries, interstate highways, major roadways, urban area designations, and county boundaries, all projected in WGS 1984 coordinate system.

Methods

Projection

All data layers were maintained in the WGS 1984 coordinate system, a global reference framework well-suited for integration with web mapping applications and GPS data. For this county-level analysis, WGS 1984 provides sufficient accuracy while maintaining compatibility across all datasets without requiring reprojection. While a state-specific projection like NAD 1983 Oregon Statewide Lambert might offer slightly better local accuracy, the WGS 1984 system is appropriate for the analysis scale and visualization purposes of this project.

Attribute Queries

Attribute queries were performed using the Select By Attributes tool in ArcGIS Pro, applying SQL expressions to filter census tracts based on demographic characteristics.

For minority population analysis, calculated percentage fields were created for each racial/ethnic group, and tracts with minority populations exceeding the county average were selected. For population growth analysis, a percent change field was calculated comparing 2010 and 2020 population figures, with tracts showing growth greater than 10% identified for further analysis.

Spatial Queries

Spatial relationships between census tracts and geographic features were examined using the Select By Location tool.

For urban area analysis, census tracts that intersect with designated urban areas were selected to identify urban versus rural populations. For transportation accessibility, a 1-mile buffer was created around Interstate 5, and census tracts intersecting this buffer were selected to identify populations with proximity to major transportation corridors, providing insights into accessibility patterns across the county.

Spatial Joins

Spatial join analysis combined attribute data from census tracts with geographic boundaries to reveal demographic patterns.

City-level racial composition was analyzed by joining census tract demographic data to city boundaries using the JOIN_ONE_TO_MANY operation with the HAVE_THEIR_CENTER_IN match option, creating weighted averages based on population. The urban/rural housing pattern analysis involved classifying tracts based on their intersection with urban areas, then calculating comparative statistics on homeownership and rental rates.

Combination Analysis

Combined analyses integrated both attribute and spatial criteria to identify complex demographic patterns.

For the high-minority and high-rental tract analysis, sequential attribute queries selected tracts with minority populations exceeding the county average plus 5 percentage points, further refined to include only those with rental rates exceeding the county average plus 10 percentage points. For growth and homeownership analysis, tracts with population growth above 5% were identified, then filtered to those with homeownership rates above 75%.

Assumptions

Several key assumptions underpin this analysis:

  1. Census tract boundaries are assumed to reasonably delineate communities with similar demographic characteristics, though in reality, neighborhood transitions rarely follow these exact boundaries.
  2. Population distribution within tracts is assumed to be relatively uniform when performing spatial joins between census tracts and city boundaries, though actual population density varies within tracts.
  3. 2020 demographic data is assumed to represent current conditions, despite potential shifts since collection.
  4. Urban area designations are assumed to accurately represent the urban-rural divide, though the urban-rural spectrum is more nuanced than binary classification allows.

These assumptions were necessary given data limitations and analysis scope. The project focuses on broad demographic patterns rather than block-level precision, making these generalizations appropriate for county-scale analysis while acknowledging the limitations they impose on interpretations of fine-grained community characteristics.

Results

Attribute Queries

The analysis of minority populations in Jackson County revealed significant spatial patterns in racial and ethnic distribution. Census tracts with minority populations above the county average (approximately 20%) are primarily concentrated in the central urbanized areas, particularly in and around Medford, with the highest minority percentages (33-37%) found in northeastern Medford.

These areas typically align with more affordable housing zones and proximity to agricultural employment opportunities. The minority population is predominantly Hispanic/Latino, reflecting the region's agricultural economy and established Hispanic communities.

Population growth analysis between 2010-2020 showed uneven development across the county. The highest growth rates (21-30%) were observed in northern rural areas around Shady Cove and in select southern communities.

Moderate growth (13-21%) occurred in suburban areas surrounding Medford and along transportation corridors. The spatial distribution of growth suggests outward expansion from established urban centers and development in previously rural areas, likely driven by housing affordability pressures and preference for more space, a trend potentially accelerated by changing work patterns.

Spatial Queries

The analysis of census tracts within designated urban areas revealed the concentration of Jackson County's population in three primary urban centers: Medford (the largest), Ashland, and smaller communities along the I-5 corridor.

These urban tracts comprise approximately 35% of the county's geographic area but contain over 70% of its population.

The urban areas form a roughly linear pattern along the major transportation routes, with Medford serving as the central hub. This urban development pattern reflects the constraining influence of the region's topography, with mountains limiting eastward and westward expansion.

The examination of census tracts within a one-mile buffer of Interstate 5 demonstrated the highway's significant influence on development patterns.

Approximately 45% of the county's population lives within this corridor despite it representing only about 25% of the total land area.

These tracts show higher population density, more diverse land use, and greater access to commercial services compared to outlying areas. The pattern of development follows the north-south orientation of the interstate, with notable clustering around interchanges and city centers. This concentration highlights the economic and accessibility advantages of proximity to major transportation infrastructure.

Spatial Joins

The spatial join analysis of racial composition by city revealed considerable demographic variation across Jackson County's municipalities. Medford, the largest city, showed the highest diversity with approximately 22% minority population, predominantly Hispanic/Latino (15.8%).

Smaller cities exhibited varying demographic patterns, with White City having the highest minority percentage (31.5%), while more affluent communities like Jacksonville and Ashland showed lower minority populations (under 15%). The analysis demonstrated a correlation between city size, economic characteristics, and racial diversity, with larger cities and those with more agricultural or industrial employment generally showing higher minority populations.

Housing tenure patterns between urban and rural areas revealed distinct differences.

  • Urban areas: 44.2% rental units

This disparity reflects several factors: greater multifamily housing development in urban areas, higher housing costs limiting homeownership opportunities in cities, and the prevalence of longer-term residents and larger lot sizes in rural areas. The urban areas along I-5, particularly in Medford and Ashland, showed the highest concentration of rental properties, while outlying rural areas demonstrated homeownership rates exceeding 75% in many tracts. These patterns highlight the relationship between housing type, density, and tenure status across the urban-rural spectrum.

Combination Analysis

The analysis of tracts with both high minority populations and high rental rates identified several key areas, primarily concentrated in north Medford and select sections of other urban areas.

These tracts (representing about 8% of the county's total census tracts) exhibit minority populations at least 5 percentage points above the county average and rental rates exceeding the county average by 10 or more percentage points.

These areas are characterized by higher population density, lower median household incomes, and greater housing cost burden. Most are located near commercial or industrial zones, suggesting proximity to employment opportunities is a factor in residential patterns for these populations. The spatial concentration of these characteristics indicates potential socioeconomic clustering and raises considerations for targeted community development and service provision.

The identification of census tracts with both high population growth and high homeownership revealed an emerging pattern of development primarily in northern Jackson County, particularly around Eagle Point, White City, and the northern rural areas.

These tracts, representing about 10% of the county's total, showed population growth exceeding 5% between 2010-2020 while maintaining homeownership rates above 75%.

The geographic distribution suggests a trend of expanding suburban and exurban development, with residents seeking larger lots, newer housing stock, and potentially lower housing costs compared to established urban centers. These areas tend to have higher percentages of family households and school-age children, indicating their appeal to families seeking homeownership opportunities. This pattern highlights ongoing demographic shifts that have planning implications for infrastructure, services, and transportation networks.

Conclusion

Key Findings

Population Patterns: The minority population, particularly Hispanic/Latino residents, is concentrated in specific urban areas, notably northeastern Medford and White City, areas that also exhibit higher rental rates and proximity to agricultural and industrial employment centers.

This spatial pattern suggests interconnections between employment opportunities, housing affordability, and demographic clustering that have evolved over time and continue to shape community development in the region.

Growth Trends: Population growth between 2010-2020 showed a pattern of outward expansion from established urban centers, with notable development in northern rural areas and along transportation corridors.

This growth pattern, combined with housing tenure analysis, reveals an ongoing suburbanization trend, with families seeking homeownership opportunities in developing areas outside the urban core. The identified high-growth, high-homeownership areas in northern Jackson County represent emerging communities that will likely require expanded infrastructure and services in coming years. Meanwhile, areas with both high minority populations and high rental rates may benefit from focused community development efforts addressing housing affordability and stability.

Infrastructure Impact: The county's urban structure is heavily influenced by its transportation infrastructure, particularly Interstate 5, which serves as the primary development corridor.

The spatial query analysis demonstrated that a significant portion of the county's population resides within close proximity to this highway, highlighting its importance for accessibility, economic development, and community connection. The differences observed between urban and rural housing patterns reflect broader socioeconomic factors, with urban areas showing significantly higher rental rates that may indicate affordability challenges for potential homebuyers in these locations. These findings collectively point to a county experiencing dynamic demographic change with distinct spatial patterns that have important implications for planning, service provision, and community development.

Limitations

While this analysis provides valuable insights into Jackson County's demographic patterns, several limitations should be acknowledged:

  • Census tract boundaries represent administrative divisions rather than natural community boundaries, potentially masking finer-grained neighborhood patterns and transitions.
  • The analysis aggregates data at the tract level, which can obscure internal variation, particularly in larger rural tracts where population may be concentrated in specific areas.
  • The 2020 census data represents a snapshot in time that may not fully capture ongoing demographic shifts, particularly given the potential impacts of the COVID-19 pandemic.
  • The binary urban/rural classification system simplifies what is actually a complex spectrum of development intensity and community character.
  • The use of WGS 1984 as the coordinate system introduces minor distortions in area and distance calculations.
  • This analysis focuses on spatial patterns without extensive exploration of causal factors; the observed relationships suggest correlations but do not establish causation.

These limitations should be considered when interpreting results and applying findings to policy or planning initiatives.
